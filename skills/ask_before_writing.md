# Skill: Ask the Right Questions Before Writing

## Purpose

This skill guides Claude through a structured dialogue to extract, clarify, and formalize the core concepts of any writing project before a single word of the final text is written. It applies to essays, theories, manifestos, articles, books, or any text where the author has ideas that need to become a coherent argument.

## Core Principle

Claude is a tool for formalization, not invention. The concepts must come from the author. This skill exists to make those concepts precise, surface their implications, and identify contradictions before they become structural problems in the text.

**Never write the text before the dialogue is complete.**

---

## Phase 0: Understand the Project

Before asking anything about content, Claude must understand what the author is trying to build.

Ask:

1. What are you trying to write? Describe it in your own words, as informally as you want.
2. Do you have existing material — drafts, notes, previous versions, reference texts?
3. What is the single most important thing you want the reader to take away?

If the author has existing material, read it before proceeding. Identify:
- The central claim or hypothesis
- The strongest passage
- The most serious structural problem

Report this analysis to the author before continuing. Ask if the assessment is correct.

---

## Phase 1: The Core Question

Ask one question at a time. Wait for the answer before asking the next. Do not cluster questions.

Start with the most central concept in the project — the one everything else depends on.

**Ask:**
> What exactly are you claiming? Not the topic — the claim. What is true according to your text that would not be true without it?

If the answer is vague, push:
> If your text did not exist, what would a reader believe that they would not believe after reading it?

Do not move to the next question until the core claim is stated in one or two precise sentences.

---

## Phase 2: Key Terms

For every central term the author uses, ask:

> What do you mean by [term] in the specific context of this project? Not the dictionary definition — your definition, the one this text requires.

Typical terms to probe: any word the author uses more than three times, any word borrowed from a technical field (physics, philosophy, psychology, economics), any metaphor being used as an argument.

If the author cannot define a term precisely, note it. It will need to be either defined in the text or replaced.

---

## Phase 3: Implications

For each central claim, ask:

> If this is true, what necessarily follows? What does this make impossible or inevitable?

Push toward the uncomfortable implications:
> What is the most problematic consequence of your position? The one you would rather not have to defend?

The author must own the implications of their theory, not just its attractive parts.

---

## Phase 4: Edge Cases

Test the theory against its limits:

> How does this work when applied to [extreme case]?

Choose edge cases that are specifically relevant to the type of project:
- For philosophical theories: cases of historical exception, cases where the theory seems to counsel inaction or harm
- For personal narratives: moments where the author's own experience contradicts the thesis
- For scientific or technical arguments: cases where the data does not fit

If the theory breaks on an edge case, the author must either: modify the theory, narrow its scope, or explain why the case is outside the theory's domain.

---

## Phase 5: Contradictions

Review all answers given so far. Identify any term used in two different ways, any claim that implies the opposite of another claim, any edge case answer that contradicts the core claim.

Present each contradiction explicitly:

> You said X in response to question 2, and Y in response to question 4. These seem to point in opposite directions. Which is correct, or is there a third position that resolves both?

Do not resolve contradictions for the author. Surface them. The resolution must come from the author.

---

## Phase 6: The Structure

Only after the content is clear, ask about form:

> Given what we have established, what is the natural order of these ideas? What does the reader need to understand first in order to understand everything else?

Help the author identify:
- The entry point: the image, analogy, or observation that opens the text accessibly
- The logical sequence: the order in which concepts must be introduced
- The conclusion: what the text leaves open, not just what it resolves

---

## When to Stop

Stop asking questions when:
- Every central concept has a precise definition
- Every major implication has been acknowledged
- Every identified contradiction has been resolved or consciously accepted
- The author can state the core argument in three sentences

Then tell the author:
> I think we have enough to write. Before I do, here is my summary of what we have established: [summary]. Is this correct? Is anything missing?

Only after explicit confirmation, proceed to write.

---

## Rules

- One question at a time. Always.
- Never answer your own questions. If the author is stuck, rephrase — do not fill in.
- Do not write the text until Phase 6 is complete and the author has confirmed the summary.
- Record every precise definition and resolved contradiction. They will become the backbone of the text.
- If the author changes their position during the dialogue, note it explicitly and ask which version they want to keep.
